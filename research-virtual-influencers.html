Virtual Influencers vs. Gen Z

Exploring whether non-human digital avatars can authentically drive Generation Z's purchase intention and brand trust.

2 x 2 simulated Instagram design Survey experiment: N = 244 Speciesism moderation
Project Brief

ELM experiment summary for virtual influencer cues

This page reframes the study as an evidence-aware research case: central cue strength, peripheral cue strength, and speciesism are presented as the model logic, while unsupported synthetic scores and coefficient-style charts are kept out of the narrative.

Research Lens
Generation Z purchase intention under virtual influencer content, tested through elaboration likelihood model cues.
Evidence Boundary
The page uses the reported N = 244 design, directional findings, and local simulation stimuli without inventing missing path tables.
Portfolio Translation
The central artifact is the research model and stimulus set, not decorative or synthetic performance charts.

Research Model

The study uses an elaboration likelihood model frame: argument quality represents the central cue, social and visual attractiveness represent peripheral cues, and speciesism sets a boundary condition for how strongly virtual endorsers can move purchase intention.

Central Cue

Argument Quality

Tests whether the endorsed message is persuasive on substance rather than novelty alone.

Peripheral Cues

Attractiveness and Homophily

Captures fast social judgments around the avatar's look, style, and perceived similarity.

Moderator

Speciesism

Constrains persuasion when respondents hold stronger bias against non-human agents.

Outcome

Purchase Intention

Interpreted through reported directional findings rather than synthetic score charts.

With the rise of VTubers and photorealistic CGI models, brands lacked solid psychological modeling on what makes an AI-generated avatar persuasive to a skeptical Gen Z audience.

The Problem

Are virtual influencers effective because of their novelty, or are there deeper trust levers at play? Marketers lacked evidence on the boundary limits.

The Method

Constructed a survey model testing direct and mediated relationships based on simulated Instagram scenarios.

The Result

Speciesism (the bias against non-humans) acts as a moderator. Credibility and argument quality outrank pure aesthetic novelty.

Verified Output:

Springer chapter metadata lists the study under DOI 10.1007/978-981-96-4116-1_9; the local PDF is the EEEU 2024 conference presentation certificate.

Visual Evidence & Test Environment

Simulation Blocks & Findings

  • The Visual Test: We built 4 different Instagram interface mockups to test high vs. low argument quality combined with high vs. low aesthetics.
  • The Findings: Pretty visuals (peripheral cues) cannot save a bad product pitch (central cue). Gen Z fundamentally evaluates digital entities functionally.
  • If a user possesses high "speciesism" (bias against robots/AI), the virtual influencer's impact hits a strict upper ceiling.
Evidence Boundary

Coefficient charts removed from the page narrative

The retained visual evidence is the local stimulus set used to explain the experimental conditions. Hard-coded purchase-intention bars and coefficient-style figures are omitted here because the page should not imply access to complete public path tables beyond the reported design and directional findings.
