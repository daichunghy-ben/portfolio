Participatory arts research

Van Gogh workshop: co-creation and customer interest

This project studies why people intend to join participatory art workshops. The important finding is specific: value does not convert directly into booking intention. It first changes attitude toward the workshop, and attitude does the conversion work.

Research story

The page now explains the mechanism before it lists the model

Art workshops are not a passive entertainment product. Participants care about what they will learn, whether the room feels socially safe, whether materials and instruction feel reliable, and whether the experience is worth their time. The model tests how those value beliefs become intention.

1

Value beliefs

Hedonic, social, epistemic, and functional value describe what people expect to get from the workshop.

2

Attitude bridge

Those values matter because they shape attitude toward the workshop. The direct paths to intention become non-significant after attitude enters the model.

3

Practical use

The venue should sell learning confidence and social comfort, not only the finished painting or the novelty of the event.

Method

Survey design with a clear decision criterion

Respondents evaluated consumption values and attitude toward Ladder Art Space workshops. Intention was framed as participating within the next three months, so the outcome stayed close to an actual booking decision.

  • Sample: 216 valid responses from Australian art and creative communities after attention and speed checks.
  • Measures: 7-point Likert scales for consumption values and intention; semantic-differential items for attitude.
  • Analysis: measurement checks, structural paths, and PROCESS Model 4 mediation with 5,000 bootstrap samples.
  • Boundary: cross-sectional intention data cannot prove retention, loyalty, or post-visit behavior.

Plain-English model

People first ask, "Will this be enjoyable, useful, social, and new to me?" Those answers shape attitude. A positive attitude then makes joining more likely.

HV = hedonic value SV = social value EV = epistemic value FV = functional value ATT = attitude INT = intention

Findings

Learning value mattered most, but attitude carried the effect

The model explains a meaningful share of attitude and intention. The strongest value-to-attitude path was epistemic value, which points to curiosity, skill gain, and the confidence to make something.

Value to attitude

Epistemic value was the strongest driver (β = .35, p < .001), followed by hedonic, social, and functional value.

Attitude to intention

Attitude strongly predicted intention (β = .59, p < .001), with R2 = .352 for intention.

Full mediation

Indirect effects were significant for all four value dimensions. Direct effects became non-significant after attitude was included.

Application

A better workshop pitch shows the process, not only the final canvas

The strongest marketing message is not "come paint something pretty". The evidence points toward beginner confidence, supportive facilitation, and visible learning progress.

Lead with learning

Show what beginners will learn and where they will get help during the session.

Lower creative anxiety

Use process photos and supportive instructor cues instead of only polished final artwork.

Make the room social

Design seating and facilitation around light interaction, not silent individual production.

Measure return intent

Follow up after the session. This study measured intention, so retention still needs evidence.

Bottom line

People book the workshop when the value turns into a positive attitude.

That makes attitude the design target: build confidence, social comfort, and curiosity before asking for the booking.
